Bill-Pay SSO · UI/UX enhancement + DLS illustrations

A bill-pay portal refresh, built for the 30–50 banks that run on it.

Bill-Pay SSO is the portal a bank's customers single-sign-on into to pay bills, recharge, and more (whitelabelled per bank, live across 30–50 partner banks). This was a UI/UX enhancement of that experience, paired with a set of micro-illustrations contributed to the payments fintech design system to bring the biller list to life.

02 · The starting point

Functional, but flat.

The portal worked and scaled, but the experience hadn't kept pace. Biller discovery was text-heavy, categories looked alike, and the screens carried little of the polish a modern bank app sets as the baseline. The job was to lift the experience without disrupting a flow tens of banks depend on.

  • Biller discovery was a wall of text: categories and billers hard to scan at a glance.
  • No visual system for categories: nothing to make electricity, water, or broadband instantly recognisable.
  • Inconsistent surfaces: forms, lists, and detail screens didn't feel like one product.
  • Live across 30–50 banks, so any change had to be an enhancement, not a rebuild.

07 System contribution

Micro-illustrations for the DLS

The biller list needed a visual vocabulary, so the work didn't stop at screens. It produced a reusable set of micro-illustrations contributed to the payments fintech design system. One coherent line style across every biller category, drawn on a shared grid, with active and inactive states baked in.

~40 category marksOne line styleGrid-spec'dActive / inactive states
DLS · The set

One family, every category.

Electricity, water, broadband, gas, education, health, insurance, housing, travel, hotels, subscriptions: each biller category gets a mark in the same weight, corner, and detail level, so the list reads as one system rather than a pile of clip-art. Each is drawn inside a consistent artboard for predictable sizing.

DLS · States

Two states, built in.

Each mark ships in two states (an active version with the brand orange accent, and a muted grey for inactive or unavailable categories) so the list can show what's selectable at a glance without extra UI. That's what makes them design-system components, not just drawings.

Design decisions

The calls behind the lift.

01

Illustrations as wayfinding, not decoration

The category marks aren't ornament. They're how the eye finds electricity vs broadband vs gas in a long list. Designing them as a consistent family makes the list scannable; contributing them to the DLS makes that scannability reusable everywhere.

02

A sample bill beats instructions

The most common add-biller failure is entering the wrong number. Showing a labelled sample bill (pointing at the exact field) does more than a paragraph of help text, so the fix is shown, not told.

03

Enhance, don't disrupt

With 30–50 banks live, the redesign had to slot into the existing flow and information model. The lift is in clarity, consistency, and the illustration layer, not a teardown that every bank would have to re-integrate.

04

Use the empty space to cross-sell

The picker and recharge screens had room beside the task. Filling it with contextual bank offers (ATM network, savings, loans, forex) turns dead space into value for the bank, without crowding the thing the customer came to do.

08 · Impact

10 · Reflection

One honest learning.

The highest-leverage thing wasn't a screen. It was the illustration set. Polishing individual pages helps one portal; contributing a coherent, stateful illustration family to the design system raises every product that touches a biller list. When you're working at the scale of 30–50 banks, the system-level contribution is the one that compounds.
